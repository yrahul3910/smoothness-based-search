Experiment 8: SMAS-Screen — Two-Stage Low-Fidelity Budget

Date: 2026-05-20 · 10 datasets × 15 repeats

1. Motivation

The original SMOOTHIE ran 30–50 cheap one-epoch cycles, estimated β, and then fully trained only the best 5–10 configs — exploring far more of the space than a full-train-every-config search, and faster than BOHB/TPE. This experiment ports that budget structure to gradient-free trees.

Tree analog of "one epoch": a low-fidelity fit on a row-subsample of the training pool. β is still computable from the cheap tree, and on the 10k–20k datasets the subsample fit is several times faster than a full fit. The screen ranks by the SMAS composite (val-R² + adaptive β), not β alone (Exp 3 showed pure-β screening loses for trees).

2. Algorithm

Stage 1 — cheap screen (80 configs):
    n_sub = max(50, 5%·|pool|)   # floor protects tiny datasets
    for each LHS config:
        fit on a random n_sub-row subsample of the pool
        record val_R² (on a held-out 20% split) and β
    ρ = Spearman(β, val_R²);  sign = ρ≥0?+1:-1;  w = 0.4·|ρ|
    score = (1-w)·z(val_R²) + w·sign·z(β)

Stage 2 — exploit (top 8 by score):
    fully fit each survivor on the full pool; pick best by val_R²
    refit winner on the full training set; report test R²

Effective budget = 80·0.05 + 8 + 1
                 ≈ 13 full-train-equivalents
                 (screens 80 configs — 2.7× the
                  exploration of the 30-config methods).

The floor matters: on nasa93dem (59-row pool) and pom3d (320-row pool), 5% would leave too few rows to build a meaningful tree or a stable β, so both fall back to the 50-row floor — essentially a near-full fit, which is already instant on data that small.

Headline (a genuine speed/quality trade-off): SMAS-Screen runs ~1.6× faster than Fair-TPE on the large (10k–20k row) datasets, but it does not match TPE on quality — it is 0W/1T/9L vs Fair-TPE (mean R² ≥ TPE on only 1/10), 0W/3T/7L vs full-fidelity SMAS, and roughly even with Fair-BOHB (1W/4T/5L). The SMOOTHIE-style cheap-screen budget transfers only partially to trees.

6. Discussion

The speed win is real but modest — not the 3–10× of the original SMOOTHIE. On the 10k–20k datasets the 5% subsample screen is faster than TPE evaluating 30 configs at full fidelity, averaging ~1.6×. But two things cap the gain for trees that did not apply to SMOOTHIE's neural nets: (i) the floor makes the screen near-full on the small datasets, so there is no speedup there (those fits are already sub-second anyway); and (ii) a decision-tree fit is already cheap, so sub-sampling saves less than skipping epochs of gradient descent does. Notably, Fair-BOHB is faster still — it applies the same sub-sampling trick inside Hyperband, so the screen has no speed edge over BOHB specifically.

And it costs quality. Ranking configs from a noisy cheap fit is good enough to win on nasa93dem (where broad exploration matters more than precise ranking), but on the capped-ceiling POM3 and the smooth XOMO landscapes the configs differ by ~0.01 R² — below the resolution of a 5% subsample — so the shortlist sometimes drops the genuinely-best config. The net effect is a method that sits a notch below full-fidelity SMAS and clearly below TPE.

Honest takeaway. The SMOOTHIE two-stage budget is worth knowing about when wall-clock is the binding constraint and a small quality concession is acceptable, but for tree learners it is not a free lunch: the cheap-screen advantage that was decisive for gradient-trained networks largely evaporates because trees are already cheap and BOHB already exploits the same fidelity axis. Full-fidelity SMAS (Exp 7) remains the better choice when quality is the priority.
