The tie/loss counts reflect statistical caution, not that the methods perform identically.

4. Key Findings

Experiment 1: SMOOTHIE-Trees vs. Random Search

Smoothness direction is dataset-dependent: SMOOTHIE-min (flat trees, low β) reduces variance on XOMO; SMOOTHIE-max (expressive trees, high β) wins significantly on COC1000 and POM3d. Neither direction dominates universally.

Experiment 2: Smoothness as a Generalization Predictor

β-smoothness is a valid (if imperfect) signal: 7/10 datasets show significant negative Spearman ρ, confirming the "flat landscape = better generalization" hypothesis extends to decision trees on SE data. COC1000 is an outlier (complex feature interactions).

Experiment 3: SMOOTHIE-Trees vs. Sequential BO

TPE and BOHB win on all 10 datasets when allowed to use test-set feedback. The training-set smoothness proxy is weaker than direct test feedback within the same 30-evaluation budget. However, SMOOTHIE has no test-set leakage and is fully parallelizable.

Experiments 4–5: INGS — Interpolation-Guided Search

Main result: INGS variants are numerically competitive with BOHB on most datasets (mean R² within ±0.01 on XOMO, notably higher on nasa93dem and pom3d) without accessing the test set. No variant achieves a statistically significant win over BOHB at n=10 repeats; the key improvement over the original SMOOTHIE is that INGS substantially reduces variance (e.g., INGS-LHS cuts nasa93dem std from 0.284 to 0.093) and closes the gap with sequential BO.

Most consistent improvement: INGS-Multi (multi-fold final selection) reduces selection noise across most datasets. INGS-LHS (Latin Hypercube init) most reliably reduces variance. INGS-Full combines both but the CV component can hurt on larger datasets by limiting exploration breadth.

Limitation: With n=10 repeats, the statistical power is low for detecting the observed mean differences. Confirming significance on nasa93dem or pom3d would require approximately 30 repeats.
