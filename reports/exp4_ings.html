Experiment 4: Interpolation-Guided Gradient Search (INGS)

Date: 2026-05-19  |  Runtime: 109.2 min

1. Motivation

Experiments 1–3 established that SE HPO landscapes are low-β (flat), making β-smoothness a useful but weaker signal than direct test feedback (TPE wins on all 10 datasets). This experiment asks: can we exploit the low-β structure more directly, by using linear interpolation over the HPO landscape to estimate gradients and navigate toward performance peaks without touching the test set?

The key mathematical insight is that for β-smooth functions, the interpolation error is upper-bounded by a term proportional to β. Because SE data has low β, a linear interpolant is a trustworthy surrogate. Furthermore, the Hessian at any point can be read off from the interpolation residual: ∇²f(h) ≈ −2r(h)/δ², where r(h) = f(h) − L(h). A point above its linear interpolant has negative Hessian, i.e., is a local maximum.

2. Algorithm

Anti-Laplacian scoring: Combining the gradient estimate (high RBF prediction) and the Hessian correction (local maximum indicator), the score for a candidate h is: 
score(h) = 2·RBF(h) − mean(RBF(neighbors of h))
Derivation: score = RBF(h) − (δ²/2)·∇²RBF(h) = RBF(h) − [RBF(h+δ) + RBF(h−δ) − 2·RBF(h)] / 2 = 2·RBF(h) − mean_neighbor_RBF
When score > RBF(h): the surrogate predicts h is a peak above its neighbors → evaluate it. When the score range is flat (< ε): explore randomly to escape the plateau.

2.1 INGS Step-by-Step

  1. Phase 0 — Initialization (10 configs): Sample uniformly at random. Proxy = R² on a fixed 20% validation split of training data (no test leakage).
  2. Phase 1 — Iterative search (20 steps):
    1. Fit RBF interpolant (linear kernel) on observed (encoded config, val R²) pairs.
    2. Sample 40 random candidate configs.
    3. Compute anti-Laplacian score for each candidate.
    4. If max_score − min_score < 0.001 (flat surrogate): explore randomly. Otherwise: evaluate the highest-scoring candidate.
  3. Return: Best config by val R²; retrain on full training data; evaluate on test.

2.2 Ablation — INGS-grad

Identical to INGS, but the score is RBF(h) only (no Hessian correction). This isolates the contribution of the anti-Laplacian term.

4. Discussion

4.1 INGS vs. TPE/BOHB

Both INGS and TPE see information sequentially and use a surrogate model, but their surrogates differ fundamentally. TPE builds a probabilistic model over the test-set objective, effectively treating HPO as test-set optimization. INGS builds a linear interpolant over the validation-set proxy, guided by the β-smoothness bound.

The theoretical advantage of INGS over TPE: when the HPO landscape is genuinely β-smooth (low curvature), the linear interpolant is as accurate as a GP/TPE surrogate, while being computationally simpler and requiring no test-set access.

4.2 Ablation Interpretation

If INGS > INGS-grad on a dataset, the Hessian correction helps — the landscape has meaningful curvature that the anti-Laplacian can exploit. If INGS-grad ≥ INGS, the landscape is smooth enough that pure gradient following is sufficient (the flat region escape fires more often and the Hessian correction adds noise).

4.3 Relationship to the β-interpolation bound

The proxy signal used by INGS (validation R²) is richer than β-smoothness (a training-only signal) but cheaper than sequential test-set feedback (which requires N sequential train+test cycles). INGS fits on the spectrum between SMOOTHIE and TPE: it sees held-out performance (validation), but from a held-out portion of training data, not the final test set. This is a principled middle ground for low-leakage HPO.
