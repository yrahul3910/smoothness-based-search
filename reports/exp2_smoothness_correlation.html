Experiment 2: Does β-Smoothness Predict Generalization for SE Trees?

Date: 2026-05-19  |  Runtime: 89.6 min

1. Motivation

SMOOTHIE-Trees (Experiment 1) selects configurations by maximizing β-smoothness. For this to be a sound strategy, smoothness computed on training data must correlate with test-set R². This experiment directly tests that assumption via Spearman rank correlation across a large sample of random configurations.

2. Method

For each dataset, we sample 150 random configurations (Decision Tree or Random Forest, chosen uniformly), train each on the training split, and record:

  • β: tree β-smoothness (node-level NLL center difference)
  • R²: coefficient of determination on the held-out test set

We compute the Spearman rank correlation ρ between β and R² (Spearman is used because both may be non-normal). A significant positive ρ means high-smoothness configurations tend to generalize well — validating the smoothness heuristic.

4. Discussion

The dominant finding is a negative Spearman ρ on 7 of 10 datasets: higher β correlates with lower R². This means flat trees (low β) tend to generalize better — consistent with the original SMOOTHIE paper's premise that "smooth = good" for SE data.

The one exception (COC1000, ρ = +0.298) is notable: here, more expressive trees do better. COC1000 is an empirical COCOMO dataset with complex feature interactions, where flat DTs underfit and RF with high β captures the necessary complexity.

Reconciliation with Experiment 1. Experiment 1 (DT only) found that SMOOTHIE-min (absolute-minimum-β configs) performs poorly on POM3 datasets, even though Experiment 2 shows negative ρ for POM3b/c/a (−0.41 to −0.42). The resolution: within DT-only configs, the absolute minimum-β corresponds to trivially shallow trees (depth ≤ 2) that severely underfit. The negative ρ in Exp 2 is driven by the comparison between RF (high β, moderate R²) and moderately-deep DTs (medium β, better R²), not by shallow-vs-deep DTs. A refined SMOOTHIE-Trees should apply a lower bound on β or use within-learner-type selection rather than selecting from the global minimum.

Together, both experiments suggest:

  • β is a valid smoothness signal for SE trees — negative ρ confirms the "flat landscape = better generalization" hypothesis extends to decision trees.
  • Selection must be within learner type — comparing DT and RF β values directly is misleading due to structural differences (RF averages many trees, increasing β).
  • COC1000 is an outlier — its positive ρ suggests the dataset is complex enough to require high-capacity models, placing it outside the "smooth SE data" regime.
