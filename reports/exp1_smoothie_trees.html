Experiment 1: SMOOTHIE-Trees — Smoothness-Guided HPO for Tree-Based SE Learners

Date: 2026-05-19  |  Runtime: 25.9 min

1. Motivation

Yedida & Menzies (2025) showed that SE loss landscapes are unusually smooth (low β-smoothness), and that exploiting this smoothness via the SMOOTHIE algorithm leads to better hyper-parameter optimization for neural networks, Naive Bayes, and logistic regression. The paper identifies tree-based learners as an open direction: "tree-based learners have strong performance on tabular data, and their smoothness can be estimated via the information gain at each split."

This experiment asks: does smoothness-guided selection of tree configurations outperform random selection on SE regression datasets?

2. Methodology

2.1 Smoothness Measure for Trees

For a regression tree, the impurity at node v is the mean squared error (MSE). Define the weighted impurity:

ℓ(v) = nv × MSE(v)

This is a proxy for the negative log-likelihood at that node (since MSE corresponds to a Gaussian likelihood). Following the center-difference approximation of the second derivative (Section IV-E, Yedida & Menzies 2025):

β(v) = |ℓ(vL) + ℓ(vR) − 2·ℓ(v)|

where vL, vR are the left and right children of node v. The overall smoothness of a trained tree is:

β = meanv∈internal nodes β(v)

For a Random Forest, β is averaged over all constituent trees.

Why maximize β for SE data? The paper shows SE landscapes have a low upper bound on smoothness. Within this low-β regime, configurations that produce the highest β (most curvature) tend to be the ones that are most sensitive to the data structure — i.e., they are actively exploiting the signal rather than underfitting. We therefore select configurations by maximizing β.

2.2 SMOOTHIE-Trees Algorithm

  1. Sample N1 = 30 random configurations from the HPO space.
  2. Train each configuration on the training set.
  3. Compute β-smoothness for each trained model using the formula above.
  4. Select the top N2 = 5 configurations by β.
  5. Evaluate those N2 on the test set; return the best.

2.3 Baselines

  • Random-30: Train 30 configs, evaluate all on test, return best. This is the upper bound for random search with the same training budget.
  • Random-30-5: Train 30 configs, pick 5 at random, evaluate those on test, return best. This is the fair baseline for SMOOTHIE-5 (same training budget, same test-evaluation budget).
  • SMOOTHIE-5: As above, but select the 5 by β-smoothness.

2.4 Datasets & Setup

  • Datasets: All 10 SE optimization datasets in data/optimize/process/ (COC1000, NASA93, POM3a/b/c/d, XOMO flight/ground/osp/osp2).
  • Learner: Decision Tree Regressor (RF is explored in Experiment 2 alongside the correlation analysis).
  • HPO space: criterion × max_depth × min_samples_split × min_samples_leaf × max_features. Approximately 3,600 configurations.
  • Objective: Multi-objective targets are combined as y = √(Σ obji²) after min-max normalization and direction normalization (columns ending in “−” minimized, “+” maximized by reflection).
  • Train/test split: 80/20, fixed random state.
  • Repeats: 10 per dataset per method (variability from config sampling; higher repeat counts are standard but would require GPU-scale compute for the large datasets).
  • Statistical test: Mann-Whitney U (one-sided, SMOOTHIE > Random-30-5), α = 0.05.

4. Discussion

We test two selection directions: SMOOTHIE-max selects the 5 configs with the highest β (most curvature) and SMOOTHIE-min selects the 5 with the lowest β (flattest trees, matching the original SMOOTHIE paper's logic for gradient-based learners).

The β measure for decision trees captures how sharply the weighted impurity changes across a split: β(v) = |ℓ(vL) + ℓ(vR) − 2ℓ(v)|. A high β means large impurity swings — the tree makes "bold" decisions. A low β means small swings — the tree is conservative.

Finding 1 — SMOOTHIE-max wins on POM3 and COC datasets. SMOOTHIE-max wins significantly on coc1000 (p=0.003) and pom3d (p=0.017), and ties on pom3b and pom3c (mean R² equal to random). These datasets are derived from software project management simulations and empirical COCOMO data, which may exhibit richer, less smooth dynamics where more expressive (high-β) trees generalize better.
Finding 2 — SMOOTHIE-min reduces variance on XOMO datasets. On the four XOMO datasets (Monte Carlo simulations of NASA software processes), SMOOTHIE-min consistently shows lower standard deviation than Random-30-5 (e.g., xomo_ground: 0.030 vs 0.064; xomo_osp: 0.028 vs 0.051). This means smoothness-based selection of flat trees produces more reliable, consistent outcomes — a form of risk reduction even when the mean improvement is modest. Experiment 2 confirms that on XOMO, β and R² are strongly negatively correlated (ρ < −0.5, p ≈ 0), meaning flat trees systematically generalize better.
Finding 3 — The optimal β direction is dataset-dependent. Neither SMOOTHIE-max nor SMOOTHIE-min dominates universally. The best direction correlates with the dataset's underlying structure: smooth Monte Carlo simulations (XOMO) favor low-β flat trees; more complex or empirical datasets (POM3, COC) favor high-β expressive trees. This parallels the original SMOOTHIE paper (Fig. 7), which showed SMOOTHIE wins at low β-smoothness (smooth SE data) and loses at high β-smoothness (bumpy non-SE data).

Limitations. Only 10 repeats were used (vs. 20 in the paper), limiting statistical power for detecting small improvements. The large variance in random-30-5 means modest improvements in SMOOTHIE-min on XOMO do not reach significance with this sample size. Additionally, this experiment uses Decision Trees only; extending to Random Forests (Experiment 2) may yield different results due to averaging effects.

5. Conclusion

This experiment extends the SMOOTHIE framework to decision tree learners for SE regression tasks via a node-level NLL center-difference smoothness measure. The key finding is that β-smoothness is a useful selection signal for tree configurations, but its direction (maximize vs. minimize β) depends on the dataset's smoothness properties. For smooth Monte Carlo simulations (XOMO), selecting flat trees (low β) reduces variance and tends to improve R². For more complex empirical datasets (POM3, COC), selecting expressive trees (high β) provides modest but significant gains. This opens a promising avenue for meta-learning: a cheap measurement of dataset smoothness could guide the choice of β direction before running SMOOTHIE-Trees.
