Smoothness-Based Hyper-Parameter Optimization for Tree-Based SE Learners

Extension of Yedida & Menzies (2025) to gradient-free learners on SE regression tasks.

Background

Yedida & Menzies (2025) showed that SE loss landscapes are unusually flat (low β-smoothness) compared to general AI datasets. They proposed SMOOTHIE, an HPO algorithm that selects hyperparameter configurations by maximizing smoothness, showing state-of-the-art results for neural networks, Naive Bayes, and logistic regression on classification tasks.

This work extends the smoothness framework to tree-based learners (Decision Trees and Random Forests) on SE regression datasets, where the goal is multi-objective optimization (minimizing cost, effort, risk, etc.). Trees are gradient-free, so smoothness must be estimated differently — we use the node-level NLL center-difference approximation proposed in Section IX of the paper:

β(node) = |ℓ(left) + ℓ(right) − 2·ℓ(parent)|,   ℓ(v) = nv × MSEv

Core hypothesis: For SE data, configurations that produce higher β-smoothness during training also generalize better — because they are exploiting the low-noise, repetitive structure of SE artifacts rather than underfitting.

Datasets

All experiments use the 10 SE optimization datasets in data/optimize/process/: COC1000, NASA93, POM3a/b/c/d, and XOMO flight/ground/osp/osp2. Multi-objective targets are combined as y = √(Σ obj²) after direction normalization.

Experiment 3: SMOOTHIE-Trees vs. TPE and BOHB

Added in response to the question: how does SMOOTHIE-Trees compare against state-of-the-art sequential black-box optimizers? Both TPE and BOHB use test-set R² as the objective and suggest configurations sequentially (30 evaluations). For tree learners with no fidelity dimension, BOHB degrades to its BO component (TPE with fewer random warm-up trials).

Result: TPE and BOHB both defeat SMOOTHIE-min on all 10 datasets (0 SMOOTHIE wins, 10 losses vs. each). Sequential BO, by seeing test performance after each evaluation, efficiently converges to good configs within 30 trials. SMOOTHIE-min's training-set smoothness proxy is weaker than direct test-set feedback — but SMOOTHIE-min has the advantage of no test-set leakage and parallel training. See exp3_bohb_tpe.html for details (runtime: ~50 min).

Executive Summary

The β-smoothness of a decision tree — measured via the NLL center-difference approximation across internal nodes — is a useful signal for guiding hyper-parameter selection. However, unlike the original SMOOTHIE paper which found that selecting flat landscapes universally works for SE data, this extension to trees reveals a dataset-dependent picture:

  • XOMO datasets (NASA Monte Carlo simulations): flat trees (low β) generalize more reliably. The Spearman correlation between β and R² is strongly negative (ρ ≈ −0.6), and SMOOTHIE-min consistently reduces selection variance vs. random.
  • POM3 and COC datasets (agile management and COCOMO models): expressive trees (high β) provide modest but statistically significant gains. The β-R² correlation is positive for COC1000 (ρ = +0.30).

This suggests that the optimal smoothness direction can itself be estimated from data, opening an avenue for adaptive SMOOTHIE-Trees that first measures dataset smoothness and then chooses the appropriate β direction — a natural extension for future work.
